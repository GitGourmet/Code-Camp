Dr. Norman Borlaug

Dr. Norman Borlaug, third from the left, trains biologists in Mexico on how to increase wheat yields - part of his life-long war on hunger.

My Gallery

Dr. Norman Borlaug (1914-2009) was an American biologist who won the Nobel Peace Prize in 1970 for his work in developing high-yielding wheat varieties. Borlaug's work is credited with saving millions of lives and preventing widespread famine in Mexico, India, Pakistan, and other countries. He is often called the "father of the Green Revolution," and his work has had a profound impact on agriculture and food production around the world.

Timeline of Dr. Norman Borlaug's Life

  • 1914: Born near Saude, Iowa, U.S.
  • 1942: Earned a Ph.D. in plant pathology from the University of Minnesota
  • 1944-1960: Worked for the Rockefeller Foundation's Cooperative Mexican Agricultural Program in Mexico
  • 1964: Founded the International Maize and Wheat Improvement Center in Mexico
  • 1970: Awarded the Nobel Peace Prize for his work in developing high-yielding wheat varieties
  • 2009: Died in Dallas, Texas, U.S.
